These pronounciation will mostly be taking hints from Turkish because that is where I feel most comfortable, but also from English. Inside regular braces, I will mostly give conjugations. For verbs, conjugation will be in order; imperative, present, past, past participle, present participle, passive. For names, conjugaions also apply since Norwegian has gendered nouns. The order will be singular indefinite, singular definite, plural indefinite, plural definite.

For example, we did not prononunce "d" at the end of råd above. But in the word rådhuset which means the town hall, the council hall; it is pronounced as {roodhüse'}.

2 Basic Structures of The Language

Previous chapter talked about pronounciation and now we are actually going to start learning some grammar. Let's take a sample sentence.

Jeg har alltid lest fine bøker.

We are trying to say something like "I have always read fine books" and it is not a big problem if we don't udnerstand all of it now. Basically, Jeg is "I". Lest is past perfect of å lese, which means "to read". alltid is "always". Lastly, har is "have". We already formed "I have always read…" and remaining part is just "fine books". Anyways, it does not matter. What matters in this chapter is the structure of the sentence.

In its core, Norwegian is an SVO language; subject, verb, object.

Subject Verb Object
Jeg hal alltid lest fine bøker

Subjects are, as we know, responsible for the action indicated by the verb. They are generally given as nouns, meaning substantiv in Norwegian, or as pronouns (pronomen in Norwegian) as in "Jeg" here. Verbs are the words that convey an action, as you know and they are a different class. Verb also means verb in Norwegian. And we see here with "always" that we can modify the place, time, mode etc. of a verb with an adverb. Lastly, objects are the things that get affected by the action of the verb, as a rule of thumb. They are also given by a name or a pronoun, like subjects. Here, we also see an adjective working on an aspect of the given object. (Adjectiv in Norwegian)

These five; nouns, pronouns, verbs, adverbs, adjectives are the most fundamental structures of any language. In this chapter, we will take a look at the basics of these grammatical structures in Norwegian. After that, we will say couple more about the sentence structure itself, it is not always SVO. Once we get the basics down, we will later provide a seperate chapter for each of these topics.

2.1 Nouns

Nouns are conjugated similar to English; according to being plural (flertal), singular (ental), definite (bestemt) and indefinite (ubestemt). Norwegian also adds gender conjugation to these. A noun can be masculine (hankjønn), feminine (hunkjønn) or neutral (intetkjønn).

Entall, Ubestemt Entall, Bestemt Flertal, Ubestemt Flertal, Bestemt
en bil (a car) bilen (the car) biler (cars) bilene (the cars)
en stol (a chair) stolen (the chair) stoler (chairs) stolene (the chairs)
ei kake (a cake) kaka (the cake) kaker (cakes) kakene (the cakes)
ei jente (a girl) jenta (the girl) jenter (girls) jentene (the girls)
et bilde (a pic) bildet (the pic) bilder (pics) bildene (the pics)
et hus (a house) huset (the house) hus (houses) husene (the houses)

This gender conjugation is hard, really. Unfortunately, there is no way but to memorize. "en" indicates hankjønn, "ei" indicates hunkjønn and "et" indicates intetkjønn. The best way is to also learn gender article while learning a noun. So, do not learn it as "bil" but learn it as "en bil". Other than that, the general rules are pretty simple. You add the gender article to the end, to make it singular definite, like et hus - huset. You add -er to make it ubestemt, flertal. Lastly, we add -ene to make it bestemt, flertal.

As every general rule, this also have many excetions. Observe in kaka and jenta that we don't directly add gender article -ei for female nouns. Instead, we add -a. Also, if a neutral noun is only one syllable like hus, we dont add er to make it ubestemt, flertal. For example;

et barn, barnet, barn, barnene [a kid]

I am sure there are a lot of other exceptions but that will wait for the later chapters. We will keep looking at the basics.
